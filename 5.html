Week 5 Project: Laser Cut

October 20th, 24th, and 27th

For the Week 5 Project, I was introduced to laser cutting. I created an enclosure for the alarm clock created for the Week 3 Project by using Fusion 360 and Lightburn.

❮ ❯

1. Idea

I have never used a laser cut, so this is a learning curve. For this project, I will be creating an enclosure for the same alarm clock used in the previous project. This subtractive process is new to me, and I found it a bit more challenging to come up with an enclosure idea. A 3mm thick plywood will be cut, and I need to think in terms of a collection of walls that somehow have to be attached to each other to hold the entire enclosure in place. For the attachment system, I decided to use the same one that my TA has used. Two pieces of plywood interlock each other by a part of a piece going through a tight hole of another piece.

Regarding the design, I wanted the time and 4 LED lights to be clearly visible from various angles, so I made the visible area of the face big. To control the clock, I made T-shape blocks that will help me access the three buttons located under the face. To hold the battery and to make it accessible, I made a lid that can be opened and closed. To allow the clock to be connected to the battery, which will be separated by a wall, I created two holes on the left wall so that the wire can go through it.

I wanted an enclosure that was elegant, futuristic, and round, so I sketched it in both 2D on paper and 3D on Fusion 360. On the 3D sketch, no parameter was used. In the 2D sketch, I indicated the dimensions. One of the main reasons I created a 3D sketch on Fusion 360 without using a parameter was because I wanted to experience creating a series of 2D sketches in different planes and understand the limitations of Fusion 360 without getting caught up on the detail and the dimensions.

2. Polished CAD

After getting a sense of the workflow needed in Fusion 360, I input all the parameters needed into a new file. A total of 8 different 2D sketches will be created, so I need to be careful on where I initiate the plane of the 2D sketch.

The most difficult and stressful part of this CAD is creating an attachment system. I used the existing attachment system that my TA has used. This saved me a lot of time as I did not have to invest my time in creating one, but the problem was installing it on each of the walls in the correct location. It was time-consuming to move the TA's attachment system to two different sketches and align them perfectly so that when I got to laser print the plywood and attach the two different pieces, they interlock correctly and in the intended place.

❮ ❯

3. CAD to CAM

Unlike 3D printing, where I can simply export it into STL, I have to export each 2D sketch into a DXF file, which is a vector file used in Adobe Illustrator. Each DXF file contains information on one piece or one wall of the enclosure, so each file has information on where the laser cutter should cut. I imported those 8 DXF files into Lightburn software, which takes care of CAM. Using the Lightburn, I identified a problem. Some of the vectors overlap each other, and this causes the laser cutter to cut on the same location multiple times. Hence, I had to go back to Fusion 360. To export the DXF file, I decided to create a new sketch and use the feature 'project'. In this way, I created an identical 2D sketch of the original but without duplicating lines. After some time, I had a perfect Lightburn file ready to be cut.

❮ ❯

4. CAM to Laser Cutter

On the Lightburn, I set the power of the laser cutter to max (100%) and the speed to 20mm/s. After a quick self-calibration, it was lasering into the plywood, burning it. After 7 minutes, it was all cut. It was cool seeing it cut and finished in less than 10 minutes, which is much faster than 3D printing, an additive process. The edges of the pieces were ashy, and the plywood had a smell of its burn.

❮ ❯

5. Final Product

Putting the pieces together was a bit of an effort, because I made the attachment a bit too tight, so I had to push them in with some force. Overall, the design is as elegant as it was intended. One of the problems was that the wire could not go through the two holes, and the hole for the clock was not placed in the correct location. Secondly, the cut for the face is a bit too low. I should have cut it a bit higher. Thirdly, the T-shape buttons can be better by being bigger so that they can be pressed more easily. Nevertheless, the final product is well made. No glue was needed to assemble it, and the attachment system holds up very well. The lid of the enclosure opens and closes elegantly. Pressing the button is satisfying with its haptic and its vibration. I am happy with how everything has turned out. If I had more time, I would love to make the minor adjustments in the parameters and print it in acrylic.
